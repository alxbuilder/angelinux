Owning the net


The start of trouble

There was a time when the mere suggestion of what's below would have (and did) attract derision and cries of paranoia. Back at time of the launch of Windows XP, Microsoft strenuously denied suggestions that those boxes phoned home on encrypted connections to Microsoft servers, reporting usage and other statistics.

Around that time, a lot of activism focused around the introduction of DRM which was either tagged as Digital Rights/Restrictions management, depending on who you asked.


A job to do

Back then it was all about the starving artist. This was the latest defense of the Home taping is killing music, and it's ILLEGAL argument. While there was a possible justification for keeping artists fed, there is no justification for embedding DRM in HTML5. Content providers never explained this to the masses before devices started shipping to the masses from the likes of Apple, Tivo, Amazon and others.

What Digital Restrictions Management Facilitates

The real problem with this technology is what it facilitates, and what it could be used for in the future, rather than what it does right now, although that situation is changing fast as the Windows kill switchshows. Data rot is the control mechanism of the future.

  • Multimedia The media will only play on an approved device. So, who approves and on what criteria? In practice it meant a single device like an iPod.
  • EBooks The Amazon Kindle is the example that jumps out.

Post Snowden landscape

Fast forward a few years and now it's about prevention of terror. There was a time when suspicions that encrypted communications were siphoned before sent were dismissed, not anymore. These practices are both revealed and analysed.

It is also well documented that before releasing security patches, Microsoft first pass data from the exploit to the NSA and it's not just Microsoft.

More is known about us than has ever been known, and it's being used NOT for security but to plant web beacons for marketing.

  • CAPTIVATEDAUDIENCE: Take over a computer’s microphone and record conversations
  • GUMFISH: Covertly take over a computer’s webcam and snap photographs
  • FOGGYBOTTOM: records logs of Internet browsing histories and collects login details and passwords
  • GROK: Log keystrokes
  • SALVAGERABBIT exfiltrates data from removable flash drives that connect
The implants can enable the NSA to circumvent privacy-enhancing encryption tools that are used to browse the Internet anonymously or scramble the contents of emails as they are being sent across networks.
That’s because the NSA’s malware gives the agency unfettered access to a target’s computer before the user protects their communications with encryption.
