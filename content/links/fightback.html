Invasion of privacy is wrong

Whether on your phone or on your computer, marketers want to as much as possible about you. More comprehensive information about what you can do, see this, and play your part stopping them shoving their noses where they don't belong...

The "I'm doing nothing wrong" arguement

Knowledge is power. The Electronic Frontier Foundation have put together a playlist of tutorials.

  • At best, your digital habits could be used to present (or withhold) discounts in online shops.
  • Your interest in a particular medicine could influence your next health insurance bill.
  • Everything else I can think of is more sinister, but you get the idea.

Let's get started...

For the time-starved, use this propublica post as a starting point. Everybody's browsing and habits are different, so balance what privacy you want with what inconvenience you are prepared to experience while you accustomise. Here are my recommendations:

Noscript

http://noscript.net/

Finding a phone number online should have been a quick, simple conversation between two parties, but so many third parties butted in, analysing. Eliminate these delays by disabling these analytics for a faster experience.

My tip for using this is to analyse what's being blocked, especially when visiting a frequently used site for the first time. Youtube thumbnails, for example, won't show until you allow ytimg which, when you think about it, means Youtube images. Expect similar on other sites, but the result is well worth it.

Privacy Badger

https://www.eff.org/privacybadger

There is a setting in your browser called do not track which you can ask websites not to track you. This is a request, not a decree or order, and will be ignored by nasty sites.

The privacy badger will impose a penalty on sites that ride roughshod over your request, and blacklist the offending sites, so you disappear from their radars.

Ghostery

http://ghostery.com/

Especially for phones, this is is an alternative to the noscript and requestpolicy that I use, and many users will probably find it simpler to use than using two tools.

The purpose of this utility is to track the trackers, so you need have no qualms about information collected, it is for your benefit.

MaskMe

https://www.abine.com/maskme/

This gets around the problem of nosey websites asking for an email address or phone number you don't want to give. For registered users, it acts as a proxy, supplying a generated value to these sites.

As a bonus, it will generate and remember secure passwords for you. There are options to view the secure passwords, sync them for use on multiple browsers or devices.

Https Everywhere

https://eff.org/https-everywhere/

Clear text is like a postcard. Whoever sees it can read it. That's http. The s is for secure so this can't happen. Deciding what to share and with whom, is a decision that should be made by you, not by others.

What you do in the privacy of your own home is not the business of a peeping Tom that decides to look in your window. What you do on your computer is not the business of the next online peeping Tom that wants to sell you something through Targetted advertising.

There are versions available for all the major browsers, which isn't true about the other tools here which are primarily for Gecko browsers like firefox.

Request Policy

https://addons.mozilla.org/en-US/firefox/addon/requestpolicy/

Tame those websites that redirect who they want to where they want, to run javascript for God knows who, or for what goal. With this, each permission to redirect has to be explicitly granted, even for search engine results!

Typically I use this to block analytics and speed up page loading. It does take a little getting used to, but in my opinion, it's well worth the effort.
